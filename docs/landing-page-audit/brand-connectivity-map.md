# Brand Connectivity Map

## Scope
This document explains how Swalook’s landing page, blog, CRM surfaces, and backend contracts should feel like one brand and one product story. It identifies alignment points, messaging gaps, and what is safe to market now versus later.

## Findings

### Current brand narrative
The current public narrative is centered on:
- revenue growth,
- retention,
- fewer no-shows,
- better marketing,
- simpler operations,
- and multi-branch control.

That is a strong direction and it fits the product surface well.

### What works
- The landing page already speaks in the language of growth and retention.
- The feature pages map to real operational workflows.
- The backend route surface confirms that the product extends beyond simple appointment scheduling.
- The CRM monorepos already point to a serious operational SaaS system, not a thin brochure site.
- The blog can support education and evaluation if it is aligned with product reality.

### What is missing
- A single canonical narrative that ties all surfaces together.
- A contract-backed claims inventory.
- A “market now vs future state” label for features.
- Explicit naming consistency across marketing, frontend, and backend.
- A clearer bridge from landing-page promise to CRM operational proof.

## Brand story framework

### Core identity
Swalook should be presented as:
- a beauty-industry growth platform,
- a revenue generation engine for salons and wellness brands,
- and a connected system for retention, marketing, appointments, billing, inventory, and performance.

### Supporting brand themes
1. **Grow revenue**
   - repeat visits
   - reactivation
   - loyalty
   - campaign automation

2. **Reduce friction**
   - appointment management
   - billing
   - operational coordination
   - staff scheduling

3. **Improve visibility**
   - dashboards
   - branch comparisons
   - staff performance
   - analytics

4. **Scale with control**
   - multi-branch workflows
   - better data visibility
   - centralized operations
   - consistent customer experience

## Surface-by-surface alignment

### 1) Landing page
Role:
- first brand impression
- narrative compression
- primary conversion layer

What it should say:
- Swalook helps beauty businesses grow by improving retention, reducing no-shows, and simplifying operations.

What it should not do:
- claim generic CRM value without product specificity
- overstate unsupported features
- feel disconnected from actual product surfaces

### 2) Blog
Role:
- education, trust, search acquisition, and product priming

What it should say:
- Swalook helps salons solve actual business problems through practical tools and workflows.

What it should not do:
- read like unrelated SEO filler
- repeat the same homepage copy in article form
- drift into topics that do not support product discovery

### 3) CRM frontend
Role:
- operational proof
- day-to-day product experience
- sales enablement
- customer retention through utility

What it should say:
- Swalook is not just marketing language; it is a real business system with functional workflows and controls.

What it should not do:
- introduce terminology that the landing page never uses
- expose inconsistent naming across modules
- market aspirational features as if they already exist unless verified

### 4) Backend
Role:
- contract source of truth
- capabilities inventory
- product truth layer

What it should say:
- the backend confirms the scope of what Swalook can truly support now.

What it should not do:
- be treated as a hidden system unrelated to marketing
- be overpromised in public copy
- drift from frontend and blog terminology

## Safe-to-market framework

### Market now
These themes are clearly supported by the current public surface and backend route inventory:
- salon CRM and management
- appointment scheduling
- dashboard and analytics
- invoicing and billing
- inventory management
- marketing templates / marketing automation concepts
- staff and attendance
- loyalty and retention
- inquiry and lead handling
- mobile app positioning

### Market carefully
These claims should be checked against actual implemented flows before strong promotion:
- advanced automation depth
- multi-branch analytics sophistication
- specific AI-driven claims
- customer-facing mobile booking claims
- any language that suggests fully finished omnichannel workflows

### Future state
These can be part of roadmap language but should be labeled aspirational:
- comparison pages against competitors
- city-by-city landing page ecosystem
- dedicated walkthrough/demo media
- case studies with verified metrics
- richer content ops automation
- any unsupported AI/assistant capabilities

## Naming consistency guidance

### Preferred naming principles
- Use one product name: Swalook.
- Use “salon CRM” when the page is about core product discovery.
- Use “salon management software” when the page is about broad operational control.
- Use “revenue generation engine” as the positioning layer, not as the only descriptor.
- Use “feature” for product capabilities and “page” for content routes.
- Keep customer-facing nouns simple and benefit-focused.

### Terms to standardize
- Appointment scheduling
- Dashboard
- Analytics
- Billing / Invoices
- Inventory
- Staff / Attendance
- Loyalty / Retention
- Marketing automation
- Inquiry / Lead management
- Mobile app

### Terms to avoid unless proven
- vague AI claims
- generic “all-in-one solution” without context
- unsupported “enterprise” claims
- marketing language that cannot be mapped to a real flow

## Product narrative by funnel stage

### Awareness
What the user should learn:
- Swalook helps beauty businesses grow and run better.
- It is built for salons, spas, studios, and multi-branch brands.

Best surfaces:
- homepage
- blog
- about page

### Consideration
What the user should learn:
- the platform covers key operational needs.
- the product is broader than just appointments.
- the system supports retention and revenue growth.

Best surfaces:
- feature hub
- feature pages
- comparison-oriented blog posts

### Decision
What the user should learn:
- this is the right fit for their business.
- the product can solve their specific pain point.
- the team or product is credible.

Best surfaces:
- demo page
- free trial
- contact
- FAQ
- blog CTAs

### Retention and expansion
What the user should learn:
- the product continues to create value after onboarding.
- the system can scale with the business.

Best surfaces:
- CRM UI
- dashboards
- branch pages
- analytics pages
- future case studies

## Messaging guardrails

### Do
- tie every promise to a product function
- keep the brand premium but practical
- emphasize revenue impact and operational clarity
- use consistent CTA language across the site
- let the product surface inform the content strategy

### Don’t
- make the homepage sound like a different company than the backend suggests
- add marketing claims that the CRM cannot support
- overdesign the story so it feels abstract
- split the brand into separate “website”, “blog”, and “app” personalities

| Surface | Primary story | Supporting proof | Conversion role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Homepage | Swalook grows salon revenue | retention, no-shows, operations, feature breadth | Primary entry and demo routing |
| Feature hub | What Swalook does | feature grid and page links | Product exploration |
| Feature pages | How one capability works | page-specific feature detail | Intent capture and proof |
| Blog | Why the problem matters | educational guidance and examples | SEO acquisition and nurture |
| CRM frontend | The product in action | workflows, dashboards, and tasks | Customer proof and sales enablement |
| Backend | The contract source of truth | routes, schemas, and capabilities | Truth layer and claim validation |

## Recommendations

### P0
- Align all public-facing copy to one brand narrative.
- Use the backend and CRM as the source of truth for product claims.
- Establish a safe-to-market rule for future feature language.

### P1
- Create a glossary of preferred product terms.
- Add product-backed proof to the landing page and feature pages.
- Use blog content to support the same narrative, not a separate one.

### P2
- Add case studies and comparison pages once evidence is strong enough.
- Expand into location-based pages only with localized support.

## Priority
High. Brand connectivity determines whether the site feels like one product or several disconnected assets.

## Dependencies
- master roadmap
- backend contract inventory
- CRM frontend product inventory
- content team approval
- final page hierarchy

## Risks
- inconsistent naming across surfaces
- marketing claims outrunning product reality
- fragmented story across blog, landing page, and app
- confusing users with too many product labels

## Next steps
1. Use the backend contract map to confirm claim boundaries.
2. Use the CRM product map to align public copy with product workflows.
3. Apply the glossary to landing page, blog, and feature pages.
4. Keep future roadmap items clearly labeled as future state.
